Using AI Personas to Pressure-Test Product Stories Before Engineering Touches Them

Chris Phelan  ·  Senior Technical Product Manager

One of the most expensive problems in product development isn't bad code — it's late feedback. Requirements that are too vague or too elaborate, customer objections that surface after a demo, assumptions about user behavior that nobody challenged until it was already built.

I started using AI personas to solve this before it happens.

The approach is straightforward: before I bring a story or a scoping document to a stakeholder, I run my assumptions through two fleet-specific personas — a Fleet Manager evaluating whether a feature would actually get released into their operation, and a Master Technician assessing whether it would hold up across different stages of a technician's career and technical reality.

The personas ask the questions a busy stakeholder doesn't have time to walk me through from scratch.

When I was scoping a Work Order system for a fleet that didn't have one, I used the Fleet Manager persona to build a working assumption set — what features a work order system typically needs, who interacts with it, what the key touchpoints are. By the time I got to my actual stakeholder, I wasn't starting from zero. They could validate and refine instead of educate. Their feedback became "we need those things, but this one doesn't really apply in a fleet environment" — which is exactly the conversation you want to be having at that stage, not after a demo.

The result is tighter requirements, fewer surprises in test, and stakeholder time that actually counts.

The personas work best as a starting point, not a final filter. They don't replace customer conversations — they make those conversations more productive by reducing the time spent on foundational assumptions. Where this breaks down is highly specialized or operationally unique fleets — a niche refrigerated carrier or a construction equipment fleet operates differently enough that generic fleet personas miss important nuance. In those cases the persona gets me close, but the stakeholder gap-fill is more substantial.
